Team 1, Sprint 7 — A Contribution Report Case Study

A real, evidence-heavy account of what happens when a peer accountability report meets independent verification. Built for future DT3 cohorts, the Sprint by Sprint paper, and the AI Playbook — updated as more team members respond.

Why This Case Study

Most contribution-report exercises produce a document, a grade, and nothing anyone remembers a month later. This one is different because almost every step was independently checked against a different source — GitHub, Discord, FigJam's own author metadata — rather than taken on anyone's word, including the report author's. What emerged wasn't a simple story of good students and bad ones. It was a case study in exactly the failure modes Scrum is designed to catch: an invisible-work tracking gap, a single-reviewer bottleneck, a discouraged team member who stopped contributing to a process rather than the team, and a student who quietly gamed a validation check. Every one of those has a name and a number in the 10 Scrum Principles reference — this document is where the theory meets a real, messy, still-unfolding case.

The goal stated explicitly by the instructor: this is not "gotcha" material. It's raw material for teaching the next cohort what these principles actually look like when they succeed and fail in a live team, plus source evidence for the Sprint by Sprint paper and the AI Playbook.

Mapped to the 13 Scrum Principles

The course's own reference lists 10 core principles plus 3 additional ones specific to the Scrum Master role. This case touches nearly all of them, with a real citation behind each — not a forced fit.

PRINCIPLE 02
Done means done. Not mostly done.

Zaw Latt was stuck on the identical PR for three consecutive weeks without incorporating feedback — a task that should have been returned to the backlog in Week 5, not carried silently for two more. Ming Hao's Week 6 presentation never happened at all, which is the same failure in a different medium: a not-done item quietly absorbed into the sprint as if it were.

PRINCIPLE 04 & 11
A blocker surfaced Wednesday can be resolved. Sunday cannot.

Zaw Latt went silent instead of flagging he was struggling, and named this himself as the core failure in his own accountability letter. The same principle explains the report's biggest blind spot in reverse: Georgii had no Wednesday-equivalent check for Figma work at all — his only methods were "check who places notes on Figma" and "check if presentation is done," both end-of-week, both Sunday-shaped. The Scrum Master's tooling never caught up to the team's actual work distribution.

PRINCIPLE 06
Velocity is an outcome — you can't optimise for it without gaming it

Zaw Latt validated his own agent output against itself — "testing agent output with agent output, of course it succeeds" — a textbook case of gaming a check to manufacture the appearance of progress rather than doing the work the check exists to verify.

PRINCIPLE 07
The demo either runs or it doesn't

Ming Hao's Week 6: no presentation took place, because the underlying tracked work was incomplete. Per the principle, that's not a partial credit situation — an incomplete item goes back to the backlog, not onto the stage as "nearly done."

PRINCIPLE 08
The retrospective is where the team gets better — not the pipeline

The Discord exchange between Shary and Georgii is, functionally, an unscheduled retrospective — and it produced exactly what a good one should: Georgii stating plainly "I appreciate you coming up with this, I will add the things I missed," a concrete admission of what broke (his tracking method), and a specific fix offered (adding her context to the report). The principle's own line — "every team member speaks, not just the loudest voices" — describes precisely what had been missing for six weeks.

PRINCIPLE 10
Sustainable pace — the sprint that breaks you helps no one

Georgii is reviewing or authoring nearly all 90 of the team's pull requests, carries 51% of total commits, and the team still has the lowest overall commit volume of any of the five teams. That's not a strength metric — it's the exact pattern the principle warns about, and it's already showing in the data independent of anyone's individual score.

PRINCIPLE 12
Dependency collapse is a Scrum Master failure — not a team member failure

The instinct throughout the original report is to name the person who didn't deliver. The principle reframes it: when Shary's work went untracked for six weeks, the correct diagnosis isn't "Shary's work is invisible," it's "the Scrum Master had no individual, documented check-in method for work that doesn't touch GitHub." Georgii's own words confirm this reading — he frames it as his tracking gap, not her failure, once asked directly.

PRINCIPLE 13
The mid-week check-in is individual, documented, and acted upon

This is the single clearest root cause in the entire case. A group "how's everyone doing" in the team channel doesn't count, per the principle — and that's effectively all that existed for non-GitHub work. The fix Georgii proposes (adding what he missed to the report) is a reactive patch; the actual principle-level fix is an individual, documented check-in that covers Figma/presentation work the same way standups cover code.

PRINCIPLE 05
The daily standup is the heartbeat of Scrum

Whitley is the one running the weekly reporting thread this entire case study is built from — the best-documented Scrum discipline of anyone on the team, even though her own commit volume is low. Output volume is the wrong lens for this role; consistency of cadence is the right one, and by that measure she's the strongest performer in the team on this specific principle.

PRINCIPLE 01
A sprint goal is a commitment, not a description

The team's own retrospective note names Week 6 as "our weakest week" with little work landing in GitHub and the presentation not raised early enough — a symptom of a sprint goal that was never concrete enough to know, mid-week, whether it was on track. Worth checking with R1 what that week's written sprint goal actually said.

Principles 03 and 09 (timeboxes, mid-sprint interruptions) don't yet have direct evidence in this case — worth watching for as more responses come in, rather than forcing a citation that isn't really there.

Individual Evidence Profiles

Full rubric with all 11 members, weighted scoring, and per-person evidence lives in Team1_Contribution_Rubric_Sprint7.docx (kept as a separate working document, updated as responses arrive). Summary of the two fully-resolved cases so far:

Shary — from "flagged, unscored" to independently verified

The strongest illustration in the whole case of why verification matters more than either self-report or peer-report alone. Three independent layers ended up agreeing: her own detailed account, FigJam's author metadata (which she did not control and could not have faked after the fact), and Georgii's own confirmation that his negative label applied to one week only. The case for teaching this: a "the report says I don't know" verdict is not evidence of low contribution — it's evidence of a measurement gap, and the two look identical until someone checks.

"I write 'Bad work' in red if person has definitively not done enough. You have that only on your first [week]." — Georgii, Discord, 19 July

Zaw Latt — the clean counter-example

Where Shary's case is about a measurement gap, Zaw Latt's is a real, undisputed performance gap — and worth keeping in the case study precisely because he didn't contest it. His letter disputed zero citations, named the cause honestly, and set concrete forward commitments. Pedagogically, this is the "this is what accountability actually looks like, and it doesn't erase the record, it changes what happens next" example.

The Structural Problem Underneath All of This: Skill Heterogeneity vs. a Single Visibility Channel

Every specific incident in this case — Shary's invisible Figma work, the "I don't know" gap, Georgii's bottleneck — is a symptom of one underlying structural condition, and it's worth naming directly because it will recur in every mixed-skill team, not just this one.

Teams like this one are not skill-uniform. Some members arrive strong in programming and GitHub-native workflows; others don't, and both the team and those members know it early. What happens next is rational and, on its face, generous: the weaker coder self-selects — or is reassigned — toward work that won't create a bottleneck for people relying on their output. Documentation, presentation, design, coordination, testing by hand instead of by script. This is often exactly the right call for the team's velocity. Shary explicitly describes this happening to her by Week 4–5, and it is very likely happening quietly in every other team in the cohort too.

The problem is not the reassignment. The problem is that the tracking apparatus every team defaults to — commit graphs, PR counts, tag cadence — was built to see code, and code only. The moment a member's contribution shifts into a different medium, they don't just do different work; they become statistically invisible to every automated signal the Scrum Master, the instructor, and the dashboard all rely on by default. A student can be fully engaged, on-task, and delivering real value every week, and the only artifact of that work visible to the standard tooling is a near-zero commit count that looks identical to someone who simply stopped showing up.

Why this matters more than it looks like it does

This isn't a measurement inconvenience — it actively punishes the team's own good decision-making. A team that wisely routes a weaker coder toward documentation or design is rewarded, under a commits-only lens, with what looks like a slacker on the roster. The incentive this creates is perverse: it tells students that the safe move is to keep grinding on code they're bad at, badly, rather than move to work they're good at, well — because only the first path is legible to the grading process. Left unaddressed, the tracking gap doesn't just misjudge one sprint. It teaches students the wrong lesson about where their effort is worth spending.

Georgii's own words are the clearest possible statement of the mechanism: "I write 'I don't know,' because all your work is outside of GitHub and I have no way to track it." That is not a complaint about Shary. It's an honest admission that the tool he had — his own eyes on a commit graph — was the wrong instrument for the work being measured, and he had no other instrument standing in reserve.

The fix is not "check Figma too." Figma has a version history precisely because design tools anticipated this problem; the same is true of most non-code collaboration tools (Google Docs revision history, Notion's page history, Miro's board activity log). The fix is a documented individual check-in that is medium-agnostic — Principle 13's actual point — where "what did you deliver" is asked and recorded the same way regardless of whether the answer is a commit hash or a board link. A Scrum Master's job was never actually "watch the commit graph." It was always "know, individually, what every person on the team did this week" — GitHub just happened to make that easy for one kind of work and effortful for every other kind.

A second confirmed instance: mentorship is invisible too, not just design work

A defense package submitted by Henry — treated as testimony, then independently checked, same as Shary's and Zaw Latt's — surfaced a Discord DM thread from 21 June: Saiko asked Henry for help with the Human Score task, Henry responded within the hour, and the two spent a Discord voice call together that Discord itself logs as lasting 4 hours. GitHub confirms it produced a real result: PR #66 was opened by Saiko at 23:58 SGT and merged six minutes later — matching the Discord timeline almost exactly. Henry received no commit, no co-author line, no PR mention. The entire contribution was recovered only by cross-referencing a private DM log against a commit timestamp days later, for an unrelated dispute.

This generalizes the structural point further than "code vs. design." Any real work that happens in a synchronous, private channel — a voice call, a phone call, an in-person session — is invisible to asynchronous, text-based tracking, regardless of whether the output is code, a design board, or a document. The tool doesn't just fail to see a different medium; it fails to see a different mode of collaboration entirely.

Georgii's own Week 5 self-written retrospective — recovered from the same evidence package — makes the same point from the other side, in his own words: "a lot of communication still is in DMs, it should be tested properly." He identified this exact gap himself, weeks before it became relevant to anyone's individual score.

First-person evidence for Principle 10 (sustainable pace), in Georgii's own words

Previously this case cited the commit-concentration data (51% of the team's commits, reviewing 88 of 90 PRs) as indirect evidence of an unsustainable load. Georgii's own submitted retrospectives make it direct. Week 5: "Not enough time, as most work is again done on the weekends... Development team doesn't have enough competent developers... Testing is done in poor and careless manner." Week 6: "I am busy and tired... What went well during this sprint? Basically nothing." This is a Product Owner describing his own burnout, unprompted, in a document meant to track the team — not something inferred from a chart.

A note on the evidence itself

Both of this update's headline findings — Henry's voice call, Georgii's own retro quotes — came from a package Henry compiled to defend himself. It happened to contain evidence that complicated more than one person's story, including the report's own author. Worth remembering when weighing any single party's submitted evidence: the most useful material is often not the part that helps the person who submitted it.

Open Tensions Worth Keeping in the Write-Up

Discouragement vs. non-contribution look identical from the outside. Shary stopped doing PR reviews after her comments were repeatedly overridden. From a pure activity count, that's indistinguishable from someone who simply stopped trying. Only her direct account — and Georgii's validation of it afterward — separates the two.

A Scrum Master's honest "I don't know" reads as a negative signal even when it isn't one. The original report's ambiguity around Shary was more damaging to her than an actual negative citation would have been, because ambiguity invites the worst assumption by default.

The report's author is also a subject of it. Georgii wrote most of the original report and scores highest in it. Both can be true and fair at once — his high score rests on externally-checkable metrics (commit count, review count), not his own narrative — but it's a structural conflict of interest worth naming every time this case is taught.
